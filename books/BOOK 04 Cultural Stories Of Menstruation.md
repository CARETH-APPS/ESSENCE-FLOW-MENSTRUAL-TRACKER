# Book 4: Cultural Stories of Menstruation
## Traditions Worldwide

### Introduction
Menstruation is a universal experience, yet every culture has its own way of understanding and honoring it. Some traditions celebrate it as a rite of passage, while others surround it with silence or stigma. This book explores stories from around the world, showing how women have navigated their cycles across history and cultures.

---

### Chapter 1: Africa — Celebrations and Wisdom
In parts of Ghana, a girl’s first period is celebrated with family gatherings, symbolizing her transition into womanhood. Elders share wisdom about self‑care and fertility.  
In Uganda, some communities hold storytelling circles where older women guide younger girls, turning menstruation into a shared journey of knowledge.

---

### Chapter 2: Asia — Rituals and Respect
In India, menstruation is often linked to spiritual practices. Some families mark a girl’s first period with ceremonies, gifts, and blessings.  
In Japan, traditional beliefs encouraged rest during menstruation, seeing it as a time for renewal.  
In Nepal, menstruation has historically been surrounded by restrictions, but young activists are challenging these practices and reclaiming dignity.

---

### Chapter 3: The Americas — From Silence to Empowerment
Indigenous communities in North America often viewed menstruation as sacred. Girls were given time in “moon lodges” to rest and reflect, supported by women in the community.  
In Latin America, menstruation has long been treated with secrecy, but modern movements are breaking taboos and promoting menstrual education.

---

### Chapter 4: Europe — History and Change
In medieval Europe, menstruation was misunderstood, often linked to superstition. Yet women quietly developed herbal remedies and shared knowledge in private circles.  
Today, European countries lead in menstrual equity, with campaigns for free period products and open conversations in schools.

---

### Chapter 5: Global Voices
- *Nia, 15, Ghana:* “My aunties danced when I told them. I felt proud, not ashamed.”  
- *Haruko, 20, Japan:* “My grandmother told me to rest and drink tea. It felt like care, not restriction.”  
- *Sofia, 17, Brazil:* “At school, we started a club to talk about periods. It changed everything.”  

---

### Conclusion
Cultural stories remind us that menstruation is not just biology — it’s identity, tradition, and community. By learning from these diverse practices, we can honor the past while building a future of openness and respect.
